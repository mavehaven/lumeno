Core Theory — Why Quadratics Matter
🎯
The Vertex Form Advantage
y = a(x − h)² + k tells you everything instantly. The vertex (h, k) is where the parabola turns — the minimum if a > 0, the maximum if a < 0. Standard form hides this; you'd need to complete the square to find it. Vertex form is standard form already solved.
📐
The Parameter a — More Than Steepness
|a| > 1: parabola is narrower (stretched vertically). |a| < 1: wider (compressed). a < 0: reflected over the x-axis. Crucially, a = 1/(4p) links it to the focus — the optical point where parallel rays concentrate, exploited in satellite dishes, car headlights, and telescopes.
Δ
The Discriminant — Nature of Roots
From standard form: Δ = b² − 4ac. Δ > 0: two distinct real roots (parabola crosses x-axis twice). Δ = 0: one repeated root (tangent to x-axis — vertex IS the root). Δ < 0: no real roots (solutions exist in ℂ). The discriminant is a diagnostic — one number summarises intersection behaviour.
🌍
Quadratics in the Real World
Projectile motion: h(t) = −½gt² + v₀t + h₀ — a quadratic with time. Revenue optimisation, suspension bridge cables, parabolic solar collectors, lens optics, and even the shape of hanging chains (catenary ≈ parabola for small sag) all use these exact principles. a, h, k are not abstract — they're physical constants.
