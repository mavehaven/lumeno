01 — Foundations

The Language of Chance

Understanding what probability distributions actually are — before a single formula

A probability distribution is just a recipe that tells you how likely different outcomes are. Roll a die: each face has a 1-in-6 chance. That simple description — "each outcome and its probability" — is a distribution.

Think of probability as a bag of weights. You have a bag, and inside are different coloured marbles — each colour representing an outcome. The fraction of marbles of each colour is the probability of drawing that colour. A distribution is just the complete description of what's in the bag.

Kolmogorov's Three Rules (1933)

In 1933, mathematician Andrei Kolmogorov reduced all of probability theory to three simple rules. Everything else follows from these.

Rule 1 — Nothing Negative
\[P(A)\geq 0\]
Probabilities can't be negative. "A -20% chance of rain" is nonsense.
Rule 2 — Everything Adds to 1
\[P(\Omega)=1\]
Something always happens. The total probability across all outcomes is exactly 100%.
Rule 3 — Mutually Exclusive Events Add
\[P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\]
Chance of heads OR tails = chance of heads PLUS chance of tails (when they can't both happen).

From just these three rules, mathematicians have derived centuries of probability theory. It's like how all of geometry can be built from a handful of axioms — the simplicity is deceptive.

Ω — Sample Space

All Possible Outcomes

When you roll a die: Ω = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. When you measure height: Ω = all positive numbers. This is the "universe" of everything that could happen.

P(A) — Probability

How Likely Is A?

A number between 0 and 1. P = 0 means impossible. P = 1 means certain. P = 0.5 means "50/50 coin flip". Nothing more mysterious than that.

F(x) — The CDF

Cumulative Probability

F(x) = "What's the chance of getting a value ≤ x?" For a fair die, F(3) = 3/6 = 50%. Always starts at 0, always ends at 1.

X — Random Variable

A Number from Randomness

Just a rule that assigns a number to each random outcome. "The number showing on the die" is a random variable. Its distribution tells you how often each value comes up.

There are two kinds of random quantities: things you count (whole numbers only — you can't have 2.7 children) and things you measure (any value on a scale — temperature can be 37.3°C). These need different mathematical tools.

Discrete — Counting

Probability Mass Function (PMF)

Like a height chart on a bar graph. Each bar shows exactly how probable that specific value is. You can add up the bar heights to get probabilities of ranges.

The PMF gives probability at each exact value
\[p_X(x)=P(X=x),\quad\sum_{x}p_X(x)=1\]
Every probability must be between 0 and 1, and they must all add to exactly 1 (something always happens).

Examples: Number of heads in 10 flips, number of goals in a football match, number of emails per hour, die face.

Continuous — Measuring

Probability Density Function (PDF)

Like a smooth curve where the area under it (not the height!) equals probability. The probability of any exact value is zero — you can never measure exactly 1.800000… metres tall.

Area under the PDF gives probability
\[P(a \leq X \leq b)=\int_a^b f_X(x)\,dx,\quad\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f_X(x)\,dx=1\]
The "area under the curve" between two points gives you P(a ≤ X ≤ b). The total area is always 1.

Examples: Height, weight, waiting time, temperature, stock returns.

The CDF Visualised — Normal Distribution

The gold curve is the PDF (the "hill" shape). The teal curve is the CDF — it accumulates area from left to right. When the PDF is tall (likely values), the CDF rises steeply. When the PDF is flat, the CDF barely rises.

The CDF is the most useful function in probability. Want to know P(X ≤ 1.96)? Just read off the CDF at x = 1.96. For a Standard Normal, that's about 97.5%. This is how every statistics table in history has been constructed.

A distribution's shape can be summarised with just a few numbers. The mean tells you where the centre is. The variance tells you how spread out it is. Skewness tells you if it leans. Kurtosis tells you how fat the tails are. Together, these four "moments" give you a thumbnail sketch of any distribution.

The Four Moments — What They Measure
1st Moment

Mean (μ) — The Centre of Gravity

Where would the distribution "balance" if it were a physical object? The average value. If heights are Normally distributed with mean 170cm, most people cluster around 170cm.

Formula
\[\mu = E[X] = \int x\,f(x)\,dx\]
E[X] just means "the expected (average) value of X"
2nd Moment

Variance (σ²) — The Spread

How far from the mean do values typically fall? Low variance = tight cluster. High variance = wide spread. Standard deviation (σ) is the square root — it's in the same units as X.

Shortcut
\[\sigma^2 = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2\]
"Average of squares minus square of average"
3rd Moment

Skewness — The Lean

Is the distribution symmetric, or does it lean to one side? Positive skew: long tail to the right (income distributions). Negative skew: long tail to the left. Normal = perfectly symmetric = skewness 0.

Rule of thumb
\[\text{Mean} > \text{Median} \Rightarrow \text{positive skew}\]
A few very high values pull the mean up above the median
4th Moment

Kurtosis — The Tail Weight

Excess kurtosis > 0: "fat tails" — extreme events happen more often than a Normal would predict. This is critical in finance — fat-tailed returns caused the 2008 crisis to be far worse than "normal" models suggested.

Reference point
\[\text{Normal excess kurtosis} = 0\]
Positive = heavier tails than Normal; negative = lighter

Think of it like describing a person: Mean = "they're about 170cm tall". Variance = "most people are within 10cm of that". Skewness = "but there's a small group who are very tall". Kurtosis = "and extreme heights (2m+) happen slightly more often than you'd expect".

When you take more and more samples, things start to behave predictably. "Convergence" describes the different ways a sequence of random experiments can "settle down" to a fixed answer. Some forms of convergence are stronger (more certain) than others.

Four Ways Randomness Can "Settle Down"

Almost Sure Convergence (Strongest) — "With probability 1, the values eventually get stuck at the limit and stay there." Like a ball that bounces, with each bounce lower than the last, eventually coming to rest. The Strong Law of Large Numbers says your sample mean converges this way.

Mean Square Convergence — "On average, the squared distance to the limit shrinks to zero." Useful in signal processing and estimation theory. Slightly weaker than almost sure convergence.

Convergence in Probability — "The probability of being far from the limit vanishes." It doesn't guarantee you stay close forever, but excursions become increasingly rare. The Weak Law of Large Numbers lives here.

Convergence in Distribution (Weakest) — "The shape of the histogram converges to a fixed shape, even if individual values keep jumping around." This is all the Central Limit Theorem requires — just that the histogram of standardised means converges to the bell curve.

Hierarchy: Almost Sure ⟹ In Probability ⟹ In Distribution. Also: Mean Square ⟹ In Probability ⟹ In Distribution. Each arrow means the left implies the right, but not vice versa.
⚠️ The Cauchy distribution is the famous exception. Its mean is undefined. Neither the Law of Large Numbers nor the Central Limit Theorem applies — the sample average of a million Cauchy values is still Cauchy. No convergence whatsoever.

Chebyshev's Inequality is a universal truth: no matter what distribution you have (as long as it has a finite mean and variance), at most 1/k² of the probability lives more than k standard deviations from the mean. It's not very tight, but it applies everywhere — even to distributions you've never seen before.

Chebyshev Explorer — Distribution-Free Guarantee
Chebyshev's Inequality — Works for ANY Distribution
\[P(|X-\mu|\geq k\sigma)\leq\frac{1}{k^2}\]
At most 1/k² of values can be more than k standard deviations from the mean. No assumptions about shape needed.

Imagine a classroom where the average test score is 70 and the standard deviation is 10. Chebyshev says: no more than 25% of students can score below 50 or above 90 (that's 2 standard deviations away). The Normal distribution would say only about 5% fall that far — Chebyshev is a conservative, universal guarantee.

04 — CLT & LLN

Two Profound Theorems

The Central Limit Theorem and Law of Large Numbers are why statistics works at all

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says: no matter what distribution you're sampling from (as long as it has a finite mean and variance), the distribution of the sample average will look like a bell curve — if you take enough samples. This is why the Normal distribution appears everywhere in nature.

Imagine you measure the height of 30 random people and record the average. Then do that again 2000 times. The CLT says those 2000 averages will follow a bell curve — even if individual heights don't. The averaging process smooths out asymmetry. This is why polling, clinical trials, and quality control all rely on means.

Central Limit Theorem (Lindeberg–Lévy form)
\[\sqrt{n}\,\frac{\bar{X}_n-\mu}{\sigma}\;\xrightarrow{d}\;\mathcal{N}(0,1)\quad\text{as }n\to\infty\]
Standardised sample mean → Standard Normal as sample size n grows. The red curve is N(0,1); the histogram is the empirical result.
30
2000

Try setting n=1 with the Exponential source — you see a skewed histogram. Increase n to 30 — it transforms into a near-perfect bell curve. This is the most important result in all of statistics.

The Law of Large Numbers says: the more data you collect, the closer your sample average gets to the true population mean. Take 10 coin flips — you might get 7 heads (70%). Take 10,000 — you'll get very close to 50%. The randomness "averages out".

A casino doesn't gamble. Each individual game is random — the casino might lose on any single bet. But across millions of bets, the Law of Large Numbers guarantees their edge accumulates reliably. Random on the small scale, predictable at scale.

Weak Law (Chebyshev, 1867)
\[\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu\]
Convergence in probability: being far from μ becomes increasingly unlikely
Strong Law (Borel, 1909)
\[\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu\]
Almost sure: with probability 1, the sample mean literally equals μ in the limit

Try "Cauchy — NO convergence!" Watch the paths wander forever. The Cauchy distribution has no finite mean, so the LLN can't apply. The sample mean never settles down, no matter how many samples you take. This is why the Cauchy is so important to study — it breaks our intuitions.
05 — Simulations

Randomness in Action

Watch probability theory come to life through simulation — from estimating π to generating custom distributions

Here's a clever trick: throw darts randomly at a square. A circle fits inside the square. The fraction of darts that land inside the circle is proportional to π. As you throw more darts, your estimate converges to π. This is Monte Carlo simulation — using random sampling to compute deterministic answers.

Monte Carlo Estimator
\[\hat{\pi}_n=4\cdot\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf{1}[x_i^2+y_i^2\leq 1]\;\xrightarrow{a.s.}\;\pi\]
Each "dart" (x, y) is uniform random in [-1,1]². The chance of landing inside the unit circle is π/4. Multiply by 4 to get π. Error shrinks as 1/√n.

Computers can only generate uniform random numbers (like a fair die with infinite faces). Yet we can simulate any distribution using a single trick: feed the uniform number through the inverse CDF. This works because the CDF is a probability — if U is uniform between 0 and 1, then CDF⁻¹(U) has exactly the right distribution.

Probability Integral Transform
\[U\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)\;\Longrightarrow\;X=F^{-1}(U)\sim F_X\]
Plug a uniform random number into the quantile function (inverse CDF) and you get a sample from any distribution you want.

Think of the CDF as a height chart. A random number 0.7 means "the height where 70% of the probability is below". If you pick a random percentile uniformly, the corresponding value follows the right distribution. It's like sampling from a percentile table.

You have 30 data points and want a confidence interval for the mean — but you only have one sample! Bootstrapping pretends your sample is the population, then re-samples from it thousands of times. The spread of those re-sample statistics gives you the confidence interval. No distributional assumptions needed.

Bootstrap Principle
\[\hat{\theta}^*_b=T(X^*_b),\quad\mathrm{CI}_{95\%}:\!\left[\hat{\theta}^*_{(2.5\%)},\;\hat{\theta}^*_{(97.5\%)}\right]\]
Resample with replacement B times, compute statistic each time, take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles as your confidence interval bounds.

You have 30 cookies from a batch and want to estimate the average weight of the whole batch. Bootstrapping says: pretend your 30 cookies represent the whole batch, then draw 30 again (with replacement) thousands of times and compute the average each time. The spread of those averages tells you how uncertain you are.
