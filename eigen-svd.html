2D Matrix Transformations

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Singular Value Decomposition is a fundamental matrix factorization method with applications across mathematics, statistics, and computer science. For any real or complex matrix \( A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \), the SVD is given by:

\( A = U \Sigma V^T \)

Where:

  • \( U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m} \) is an orthogonal matrix whose columns are the left singular vectors
  • \( \Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \) is a rectangular diagonal matrix with non-negative singular values \( \sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_p \geq 0 \) on the diagonal, where \( p = \min(m,n) \)
  • \( V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \) is an orthogonal matrix whose columns are the right singular vectors
Existence Theorem (Eckart-Young-Mirsky)

Every matrix \( A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \) has a singular value decomposition. If \( A \) is real, then \( U \) and \( V \) can be taken to be real orthogonal matrices.

Geometric Interpretation

The SVD reveals that any linear transformation can be decomposed into three simple transformations:

  1. A rotation/reflection \( V^T \) in the domain
  2. A scaling \( \Sigma \) along the coordinate axes
  3. A rotation/reflection \( U \) in the codomain

In 2D, the unit circle is transformed into an ellipse. The right singular vectors \( V \) give the directions of the principal axes of the ellipse before transformation, while the left singular vectors \( U \) give the directions after transformation.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

For a square matrix \( A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \), an eigenvector \( \mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0} \) and eigenvalue \( \lambda \) satisfy:

\( A \mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \)

The eigenvalues are found by solving the characteristic equation:

\( \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \)

Spectral Theorem

For symmetric matrices \( A = A^T \), the spectral theorem guarantees:

  • All eigenvalues are real
  • Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal
  • \( A \) can be diagonalized as \( A = Q \Lambda Q^T \), where \( Q \) is orthogonal and \( \Lambda \) is diagonal

Relationship between SVD and Eigenvalue Decomposition

For a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix, the SVD and eigenvalue decomposition coincide:

\( A = U \Sigma U^T \)

For general matrices, the singular values are the square roots of the eigenvalues of \( A^T A \) (or \( A A^T \)):

\( \sigma_i(A) = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)} \)
Proof Sketch

Starting with \( A = U \Sigma V^T \), we have:

\( A^T A = (V \Sigma^T U^T)(U \Sigma V^T) = V \Sigma^T \Sigma V^T \)

Since \( \Sigma^T \Sigma \) is a diagonal matrix with entries \( \sigma_i^2 \), this shows that the columns of \( V \) are eigenvectors of \( A^T A \) with eigenvalues \( \sigma_i^2 \).

Applications

  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA): SVD is used to find the principal components of data
  • Matrix Approximation: The Eckart-Young theorem states that the best rank-k approximation is given by truncating the SVD
  • Linear Systems: SVD provides a numerically stable method for solving linear systems
  • Image Compression: Low-rank approximations via SVD can compress images

3D Matrix Transformations

3D Singular Value Decomposition

In three dimensions, the SVD of a matrix \( A \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3} \) is:

\( A = U \Sigma V^T \)

Where \( U, V \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3} \) are orthogonal matrices and \( \Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3} \) is diagonal with non-negative entries.

Geometric Interpretation in 3D

The SVD decomposition in 3D has a beautiful geometric interpretation:

  1. The unit sphere \( \{ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \|\mathbf{x}\| = 1 \} \) is transformed by \( A \) into an ellipsoid
  2. The right singular vectors (columns of \( V \)) give the principal axes of the ellipsoid in the input space
  3. The singular values \( \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3 \) give the lengths of the semi-axes of the ellipsoid
  4. The left singular vectors (columns of \( U \)) give the directions of the semi-axes in the output space
Volume Transformation

The volume of the transformed unit sphere (ellipsoid) is given by:

\( \text{Volume} = \frac{4}{3}\pi \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sigma_3 \)

This equals \( \frac{4}{3}\pi |\det(A)| \), since \( |\det(A)| = \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sigma_3 \).

3D Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

For a 3×3 matrix \( A \), eigenvalues satisfy the characteristic polynomial:

\( \det(A - \lambda I) = -\lambda^3 + \text{tr}(A)\lambda^2 - \text{tr}(\text{adj}(A))\lambda + \det(A) = 0 \)

Classification of 3D Transformations

Based on eigenvalues, 3D linear transformations can be classified:

  • Three real eigenvalues: The transformation has three principal directions of stretching/compression
  • One real and two complex eigenvalues: The transformation includes a rotation component
  • All eigenvalues real and positive: The transformation preserves orientation
  • Negative determinant: The transformation reverses orientation
Visual Interpretation

In this visualization:

  • The transparent sphere represents the unit sphere in the input space
  • The ellipsoid shows how the matrix transforms the unit sphere
  • Eigenvectors (when real) show directions that are only scaled by the transformation
  • Singular vectors show the principal directions of stretching

Special Cases in 3D

Symmetric Matrices

For symmetric matrices \( A = A^T \):

  • All eigenvalues are real
  • Eigenvectors are orthogonal
  • SVD and eigenvalue decomposition coincide: \( A = Q \Lambda Q^T \)

Orthogonal Matrices

For orthogonal matrices \( Q^T Q = I \):

  • All singular values are 1
  • The transformation is a pure rotation or reflection
  • The unit sphere is mapped to itself

Rank-Deficient Matrices

When one or more singular values are zero:

  • The transformation collapses one or more dimensions
  • The ellipsoid becomes degenerate (flattened in some directions)
  • The matrix has a non-trivial null space
